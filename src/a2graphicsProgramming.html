Graphics Programming was my first real introduction to OpenGL and programming on the graphics card. I decided to make a tech demo to demonstrate my skills in OpenGL by implementing deferred rendering with HDR Bloom.

Deferred Rendering taught me how framebuffers worked as you have to render to multiple G-buffer targets. Each light then reads the G-buffers to calculate its own lighting in the world before outputting to a final buffer that is rendered to the screen.

The HDR Bloom effect that I used is the one used in Call of Duty: Advanced Warfare. This is the same one that Unity uses in its standard shader pipeline. It is made of three main steps. A prefilter, down sample passes and an up sample passes. The prefilter is responsible for capturing the areas where bloom will come from. Essentially, any pixels that have an intensity above a particular threshold.

Both the down and up sample passes are used to repeatedly blur the image while decreasing or increasing the size of the image.

Once all the passes have been completed, we can add the very blurry image back onto the original image.
